The End of the LPC - 5 Big Questions Answered

02 APRIL
by Richard Burnham

Amidst the complexity of the 2014 Training Regulations, what refreshing news; paralegals and other non-qualified members of the profession will seek solace in the fact that the Solicitors Regulation Authority have recently approved the first application for a paralegal to be admitted to the roll as a solicitor through the "equivalent means" process.

Robert Houchill, of the firm Bates Wells Braithwaite, has become the first paralegal to qualify as a solicitor by demonstrating that he achieved the same standards as he would have achieved during the traditional two year training contract period.

Houchill's four and a half years’ experience as a paralegal were deemed to have provided him with the same skills and standards that he would have obtained during a formal training contract, and as such (having already completed the Legal Practice Course) he was "excused" from this requirement of qualification.

Whilst encouraging for many, the new route to qualification is creating some stir amongst members of the profession, with many deeming the relaxation of the formal requirements as an attack on the "solicitor brand" and a reduction in the quality of those being admitted by the SRA.

To my mind the first successful application, in evidencing the demanding nature of the process, certainly supports exactly the opposite. It emphasises that the SRA are not seeking a lower standard than that obtained through a training contract, but its "equivalent" (hence the name “equivalent means”).

It needs to be stressed that there is no reduction in the standards required and one would hope that this will be understood more firmly as the scheme gains traction and familiarity with time.

With that being said, there are also concerns among the paralegal cohort that the new regulations will create a stigmatised tiered approach to qualification, creating "training contract solicitors", "paralegal solicitors" and "apprentice solicitors". However, if the 2014 Training Regulations function as the SRA intend, then this should not be the case. If the standard is met, a person will be admitted to the roll, if not, they will not. This is also the position with traditional training contracts.

The news is extremely promising, but this is just the beginning; the scheme has a long way to go before any sense of consistency or predictability is generated from the various decisions made in response to applications.

I am personally interested to see how the new qualification route complies with the standard business models utilised by law firms (and if any will adapt to accommodate it), and whether the paralegal employment market will suffer any detriment as a result of these revelations.

Ultimately this is merely the tip of the new training regulation iceberg; it wholly remains to be seen what could constitute the "equivalent means" of the other requirements to becoming a solicitor. When will we see the first qualification where a paralegal has engaged the "equivalent means" of the Legal Practice Course? Or even the Qualifying Law Degree?

This is a promising step forward for ensuring diversity in the entrance to the profession, but there is still a long way to go. With 28 other paralegals awaiting a response from the SRA regarding their applications, we should soon begin to get a clearer picture of this exciting new regulatory device in the very near future.
